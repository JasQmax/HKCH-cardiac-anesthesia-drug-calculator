⚠️ Key Reminders
  • Propofol: Reduce dose 30–50% if opioid/benzo given. Painful on injection — pre-treat with IV lignocaine. PRIS risk with prolonged infusion.
  • Etomidate: Best choice in haemodynamic compromise. Single dose → transient adrenal suppression (6–24 h). Myoclonus common — pre-treat with opioid or benzo.
  • Ketamine IN/PO: IN onset 5–10 min; PO 20–45 min. Add midazolam to ↓dysphoria. ↑Secretions — consider glycopyrrolate.
  • Midazolam IN: Burns on instillation — warn family. Max 0.3 mL/nostril.
  • Dexmedetomidine IN: Plan 30 min ahead. Minimal respiratory depression. Watch for bradycardia/hypotension.
  • Suxamethonium: Contraindicated in hyperkalaemia, burns, denervation, myopathy, MHS. Have dantrolene available.
  • Atropine + Neostigmine: Give together; ratio ≈ atropine 0.02 mg/kg : neostigmine 0.05 mg/kg.
  • Glycopyrrolate: Alternative to atropine for reversal (0.01 mg/kg); less tachycardia, no CNS effects. As antisialogogue: 4–10 mcg/kg, useful with ketamine.
  • Sugammadex: Rocuronium/vecuronium only. 2 mg/kg moderate block; 16 mg/kg deep/RSI reversal.
  • Naloxone: Duration shorter than opioids — monitor for re-narcotisation. Titrate to avoid precipitating pain/HTN.
  • Flumazenil: Short-acting — may need repeat. Can precipitate seizures in BZD-dependent patients.
  • Mannitol: Monitor osmolality (target <320 mOsm/kg). Avoid in hypovolaemia without volume replacement.
  • Hydrocortisone: Stress/crisis dose 1–2 mg/kg (max 100 mg); bronchospasm dose 4 mg/kg (max 200 mg). For periop BSA-based cover, use BSA calculator.

📋 Administration & Monitoring Guide
Administration:
  • Infuse over 15–30 minutes
  • Do NOT bolus insulin — risk of severe hypoglycaemia
  • Peripheral IV: Use D10W only (≤12.5% dextrose)
  • Central line: D25W or D50W can be used
⚠️ Monitoring — CRITICAL:
  • BGL: Every 15–30 min for 2 hours, then hourly for 4–6 hours
  • Hypoglycaemia risk: Highest 30–60 min post-infusion
  • K⁺: Recheck at 1 hour
  • ECG: Continuous if K⁺ > 6.5 mmol/L or ECG changes
💊 Other Hyperkalaemia Treatments
  • Calcium gluconate 10%: 0.5 mL/kg (max 20 mL) over 5–10 min — cardioprotection
  • Salbutamol nebulised: 2.5–5 mg — drives K⁺ intracellularly
  • Sodium bicarbonate: 1–2 mmol/kg if metabolic acidosis
  • Resonium: 1 g/kg PO/PR — removes K⁺ (slow onset)
  • Dialysis: For refractory hyperkalaemia or renal failure

  4. Defibrillate — 2nd shock, then give Adrenaline, then resume CPR:
    4 J/kg = — J Adrenaline 10 mcg/kg = — mcg → — mL
    Conc: —
  5. 2 min CPR → Check rhythm → if still shockable:
  6. Defibrillate — 3rd shock, then give Amiodarone OR Lignocaine (1st dose), then resume CPR:

    Shock energy: ≥ 4 J/kg — may escalate; max 10 J/kg or adult dose (360 J)

🫀 NON-SHOCKABLE
PEA / Asystole
  1. CPR 2 min — high-quality, minimise interruptions
  2. IV/IO access — Adrenaline ASAP, target within 5 min of arrest:
    10 mcg/kg = — mcg → — mL
    Conc: —
    ⏱️ Earlier adrenaline → improved outcomes
  3. Continue CPR 2 min → Check rhythm
  4. If still non-shockable: repeat Adrenaline every 3–5 min
  5. Treat reversible causes (4H + 4T)
  6. Consider advanced airway
No shock for PEA/Asystole. Focus on high-quality CPR, early adrenaline, and reversible causes.
🔍 Reversible Causes — 4H & 4T
4 H
  • Hypoxia
  • Hypovolaemia
  • Hypo/Hyperkalaemia & metabolic
  • Hypothermia
4 T
  • Thrombosis (pulmonary / coronary)
  • Tension pneumothorax
  • Tamponade (cardiac)
  • Toxins / drugs
✅ Post-ROSC Care
  • Avoid hypoxia (target SpO₂ 94–99%)
  • Avoid hyperventilation — target normal PaCO₂
  • 12-lead ECG; treat reversible cause
  • Glucose management (avoid hypo/hyperglycaemia)
  • Temperature: Prevent hyperthermia — maintain central temperature < 37.5 °C. Avoid hyperthermia strictly in comatose infants and children to improve neurological outcomes. Active cooling if needed; continuous temperature monitoring.
  • Haemodynamic targets: Maintain systolic BP and MAP > 10th percentile for age and sex. Use IV fluids and vasopressors/inotropes as needed to achieve and sustain these targets.

🫀 CPR Quality & Haemodynamic Targets
📌 CPR Technique
  • Push hard — ≥ ⅓ AP chest diameter
  • Push fast — 100–120 compressions/min
  • Allow full chest recoil
  • Minimise interruptions — < 10 sec
  • Rotate compressors every 2 min
🔢 Compression : Ventilation Ratio
  • No advanced airway:
    • 15:2 — 2 rescuers, prepuberty
    • 30:2 — 2 rescuers, post-puberty
    • 30:2 — 1 rescuer, any age
  • Advanced airway in place:
    • Continuous compressions
    • 1 breath every 2–3 sec (20–30/min)
📊 Haemodynamic Targets (invasive monitoring)
  • Diastolic BP ≥ 25 mmHg — infants
  • Diastolic BP ≥ 30 mmHg — children ≥ 1 yr
  • Use to guide CPR quality & vasopressor titration
Reference: AHA/PALS 2025 Guidelines — Pediatric Advanced Life Support.

Step 1 — Initial Checks: Correct Physiological Derangements
Before addressing coagulopathy, ensure:
  • Temperature ≥ 35 °C (core warming target before protamine)
  • pH > 7.2  |  Ionised Ca²⁺ > 1.1 mmol/L
  • Hb/Hct within acceptable range  |  Surgical bleeding controlled
↓

⚖️ Ideal Body Weight (IBW)

Ideal Body Weight is the weight corresponding to the 50th percentile for the patient's height and sex.
Used for drug dosing in obese patients where volume of distribution correlates with lean/ideal mass (e.g. some anaesthetic agents).

📋 IBW Formulae & References
Children (2–10 years): IBW (kg) = Age (years) × 2 + 8

🩺 Clinical Notes on Vital Signs
  • Heart rate: Tachycardia is the first compensatory sign in paediatric shock. Bradycardia is a pre-arrest sign — treat urgently.
  • Blood pressure: Children maintain BP until decompensated shock — a normal BP does not exclude shock. Assess perfusion (capillary refill, skin mottling, mental status) clinically.
  • Respiratory rate: Tachypnoea may indicate respiratory distress, metabolic acidosis, or pain. Count for a full minute in infants.
  • SpO₂: Target ≥ 95% for most children. Neonates with cyanotic congenital heart disease may have lower acceptable saturations — confirm with cardiology team.
  • Temperature: Normal 36.5–37.5 °C. Fever > 38 °C increases HR by ~10 bpm per degree.
  • Clinical context: Always consider the patient's normal range and clinical condition. Heart rate will normally increase with fever and stress.
